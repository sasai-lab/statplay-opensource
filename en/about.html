// StatPlay

An interactive visualization tool for experiencing statistical concepts firsthand. Not a replacement for textbooks, but a device for those "if only it were moving, the picture would land instantly" moments.

Calculation practice and systematic study are left to textbooks and problem sets. All this tool can do is create experiences that build understanding through imagery. If it makes you think "statistics is actually interesting," that's enough.

Content is structured with reference to the following:

  • Introduction to Statistics (はじめての統計学), Yasuhiko Torii, Nikkei Publishing
  • A Completely Self-Taught Introduction to Statistics (完全独習 統計学入門), Hiroyuki Kojima, Diamond, Inc.

// How This Tool Came to Be

StatPlay began from a self-study moment: getting stuck on standardizing to the normal distribution — the words made sense, but no mental picture would form.

"If only it were moving, the picture would land instantly." That hunch — that interactive experience might transform concept understanding in an instant — is where the project starts.

The skills to implement interactive visualizations on the frontend, however, weren't part of a direction-and-management background. In an earlier era this would have ended at "would love to build it, can't." AI happened to come along as an option, and that made it possible to ship.

AI writes the code. What to build — that stays a human call. With a direction background, reckless-looking implementations tend to surface just by watching the terminal: is the design correct, will it survive future extensions? More importantly, without a correct grasp of the statistical theory, "which variables to move so that the imagery is optimal for understanding" never comes into view. Things the builder doesn't understand can't be built correctly — StatPlay is a direct record of that fact.

Building itself turned out to be the deepest form of learning.

The deeper the learning goes, the more there is to build. And figuring out how to make it move only gets harder.

Statistics, it turns out, is genuinely fascinating.
