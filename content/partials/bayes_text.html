// ここで使われる公式

P(A|B)＝陽性→病気の確率、P(B|A)＝感度、P(A)＝有病率、P(B)＝陽性が出る確率全体。
有病率が小さいと分子が縮み、偽陽性を含む分母が相対的に大きくなる——上の「60人中10人」がまさにこれ。

// Formula used here

P(A|B) = probability of being sick given a positive test, P(B|A) = sensitivity, P(A) = prevalence, P(B) = overall probability of testing positive.
When prevalence is tiny the numerator shrinks while the denominator, swollen with false positives, stays large — that's exactly the "10 out of 60" above.

// よくある誤解

❌「陽性 = 病気」

有病率1%, 感度95%, 特異度90% だと、陽性的中率はたった 8.8%。陽性100人中、実際に病気なのは9人だけ。残りの91人は健康なのに陽性（偽陽性）。上のシミュレーションで有病率を下げていくと、この比率が目で見てじわじわ腑に落ちてきます。

❌「感度と特異度が高ければ安心」

有病率が低すぎる集団に使うと、感度・特異度が両方99%でも陽性的中率は50%程度。「誰に対して検査するか」（事前確率の選び方）が結果を大きく左右する。

❌「ベイズの定理は1回使って終わり」

1回目の事後確率を2回目の事前確率として更新できる。2回連続で陽性なら、事後確率はぐっと上がる。これが「ベイズ更新」の考え方。

// Common misconceptions

❌ "Positive test = disease"

With 1% prevalence, 95% sensitivity, and 90% specificity, the positive predictive value is only about 8.8%. Of 100 people who test positive, roughly 9 actually have the disease — the other 91 are healthy false positives. Drag the prevalence slider down in the simulation above and watch this ratio shift before your eyes.

❌ "High sensitivity and specificity are enough"

Screen a low-prevalence population and even with 99% sensitivity and 99% specificity, the positive predictive value is only about 50%. Who you test (the prior probability) matters as much as how good the test is.

❌ "Bayes' theorem is a one-shot calculation"

The posterior from the first test can become the prior for the second. Two consecutive positives dramatically raise the posterior probability. This is the idea behind Bayesian updating.

// 試験での出方

  • 定番計算：「有病率 p%, 感度 a%, 特異度 b%。検査陽性のとき病気の確率は？」
  • 全確率の公式：P(B) = P(B|A)P(A) + P(B|Ā)P(Ā) を分母に使う
  • 自然頻度で解く：1000人の表を書いて直接数えるのが確実。公式を暗記するより速い

// How it shows up in exams

  • Standard calculation: "Prevalence p%, sensitivity a%, specificity b%. Given a positive test, what's the probability of disease?"
  • Law of total probability: use P(B) = P(B|A)P(A) + P(B|Ā)P(Ā) as the denominator
  • Natural frequency approach: draw a table of 1,000 people and count directly — often faster than memorizing the formula
